# Labor Economics and Public Policy

[Source: WORK/KNOWLEDGE/Business/Labor Economics and Public Policy/ — MIT OCW 14.64, Prof. Joshua Angrist, Fall 2009]

Applied microeconomics of labor markets: supply, demand, human capital, wage structure, discrimination, and unions — with strong emphasis on causal empirical methods.

---

## Course Structure (8 modules)

1. **Introduction** — employment/earnings facts; supply-demand framework; regression + differences-in-differences
2. **Labor Supply** — budget constraint + indifference curves; welfare & EITC; intertemporal substitution; unemployment insurance
3. **Home Production & Family Economics** — Gronau model; instrumental variables; children's effect on parental labor supply
4. **Demand for Labor, Minimum Wages, Monopsony** — firm-level demand; MW empirics (Card-Krueger NJ/PA natural experiment)
5. **Human Capital, Education, Training** — schooling returns (Mincer equation); compulsory attendance IV; education production functions (class size, vouchers)
6. **Wage Structure** — inequality trends; skill-biased technological change; SBTC vs. task framework
7. **Discrimination** — Becker taste-based model; statistical discrimination; anti-discrimination policy effects (ADA, Civil Rights Act)
8. **Unions and Bargaining** — union formation; wage premium; bargaining models; strike activity

## Core Concepts

**Labor Supply**
- Neoclassical model: budget constraint (wage × hours + non-labor income = expenditure), utility maximization over consumption/leisure. Labor supply curve can backward-bend at high wages (income effect dominates).
- EITC creates three regions: phase-in (positive wage subsidy, increases supply), flat (no effect), phase-out (negative marginal rate, decreases supply). Eissa-Liebman: EITC expanded single-mother labor force participation significantly.
- Unemployment insurance generates moral hazard: Meyer (1990) uses regression discontinuity on benefit exhaustion to show UI extends unemployment spells.

**Instrumental Variables (Key Empirical Method)**
- Solves endogeneity (e.g., education is correlated with unobserved ability). IV requires: relevance (instrument correlates with treatment) + exclusion restriction (instrument affects outcome only through treatment).
- Classic instruments: quarter of birth → school start-age → compulsory attendance (Angrist-Krueger 1991); sibling sex composition → family size (Angrist-Evans 1998); Vietnam draft lottery → military service → earnings (Angrist 1990).

**Human Capital**
- Mincer earnings equation: log(wage) = α + β·schooling + γ·experience + ε. Returns to schooling ≈ 8-10% per year (OLS); IV estimates often higher, consistent with returns exceeding opportunity cost for marginal students.
- Card (1995): proximity to college as IV for schooling — geographic variation in access generates exogenous education variation.
- Class size: Angrist-Lavy (1999) uses Maimonides Rule (Israeli class-size cap at 40) as regression-discontinuity instrument. Smaller classes improve test scores.

**Wage Structure & Inequality**
- Skill-biased technological change (SBTC): demand shifts toward high-skill workers accelerate relative to supply, widening college premium. Krueger (1993): computer use associated with 10-15% wage premium.
- DiNardo-Pischke (1997): pencils also correlated with wages, suggesting omitted variable bias in computer returns.
- Katz-Autor: rising within-group inequality requires explanation beyond SBTC; task-based framework (routine vs. non-routine manual/cognitive) better fits polarization.

**Discrimination**
- Becker model: discriminating employers pay a wage premium (discrimination coefficient d) — minority worker hired only if wage ≥ W − d·W. Competition should erode taste-based discrimination; monopsony preserves it.
- Heckman-Payner (1989): Civil Rights Act caused significant Black earnings gains in South Carolina — timing rules out alternative explanations.
- Acemoglu-Angrist (2001): ADA reduced employment of disabled workers — cost of accommodation imposed by legislation exceeded wage reduction, reducing demand.

**Unions**
- Union wage premium estimated at 15-20% (H. Gregg Lewis survey). Premium varies by industry, occupation, and era.
- DiNardo-Lee (2004): regression discontinuity at 50%-vote-threshold in union elections finds small or negligible wage effects — close-election unions may be weakest.
- Ashenfelter-Johnson (1969): union bargaining model separates member preferences from leadership preferences; strikes arise when expectations diverge.

## Key Papers (Curated Reading List)

**Foundational**
- Borjas, *Labor Economics* 5th ed. (standard textbook)
- Angrist & Krueger, "Empirical Strategies in Labor Economics" (Handbook ch. 23) — IV methods taxonomy
- Angrist & Pischke, *Mostly Harmless Econometrics* — canonical applied econometrics reference

**Labor Supply**
- Eissa & Liebman (QJE 1996) — EITC and labor force participation
- Fehr & Goette (AER 2007) — randomized field experiment, wage elasticity
- Oettinger (JPE 1999) — stadium vendor daily labor supply
- Imbens, Rubin & Sacerdote (AER 2001) — lottery windfall income effects on labor supply
- Meyer (Econometrica 1990) — UI moral hazard
- Farber (JPE 2005) — NYC cabdriver daily labor supply (target income hypothesis)

**Family Economics**
- Gronau (JPE 1977) — home production theory
- Angrist & Evans (AER 1998) — children and parental labor supply; sibling-sex IV

**Minimum Wages**
- Card & Krueger (AER 1994) — NJ vs. PA fast food; difference-in-differences, no employment effect
- Angrist (JOLE 1996) — Palestinian short-run labor demand; wage floors and monopsony

**Human Capital**
- Angrist & Krueger (QJE 1991) — quarter-of-birth IV, compulsory schooling
- Angrist (AER 1990) — Vietnam draft lottery; military service and lifetime earnings
- Krueger (QJE 1993) — computers and wage structure
- Angrist & Lavy (QJE 1999) — Maimonides Rule, class size
- Card (Handbook ch. 30) — causal effect of education on earnings
- Card & Krueger (JPE 1992) — school quality and Black-White earnings

**Wage Structure**
- Lemieux (JPE 2008) — changing nature of wage inequality
- Autor, Katz & Kearney (REStat 2008) — U.S. wage inequality trends
- Katz & Autor (Handbook ch. 26) — changes in wage structure

**Discrimination**
- Heckman & Payner (AER 1989) — Civil Rights Act impact on Black economic status
- Acemoglu & Angrist (JPE 2001) — ADA and disability employment

**Unions**
- Lewis, *Union Relative Wage Effects* (1986) — comprehensive wage premium survey
- DiNardo & Lee (QJE 2004) — union election RD, economic impacts
- Ashenfelter & Johnson (AER 1969) — bargaining theory and strikes
