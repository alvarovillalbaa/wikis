# The Lean Startup

[Source: WORK/BOOKS/The Lean Startup - Erick Ries.pdf]

**Central thesis:** Startups exist under conditions of extreme uncertainty; the goal of early-stage work is not to execute a plan but to learn what to build as fast as possible. Learning must be validated — not assumed.

## Validated Learning

The fundamental unit of startup progress. A validated learning event = a rigorously tested hypothesis about what customers value. Not anecdote, not intuition — an experiment with falsifiable outcomes.

**Build-Measure-Learn (BML) loop** — the core engine:
- **Build** → produce an MVP to test a specific hypothesis
- **Measure** → collect actionable metrics against that hypothesis
- **Learn** → decide whether to pivot or persevere

Speed through the BML loop is the primary competitive variable in the early stage. Each cycle either confirms or eliminates an assumption.

## Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

The smallest artifact that allows one pass through the BML loop. Not the smallest product, not a prototype — a learning instrument. The goal is maximum validated learning with minimum effort.

**Three MVP failure modes:**
1. Too polished — feature completeness delays learning
2. Wrong metric — measures vanity (page views, downloads) not behavior
3. No hypothesis — no falsifiable claim being tested

**Concierge MVP:** fulfill the product manually before automating. Zappos didn't build inventory software — Aaron Patzer photographed shoes, fulfilled orders by hand. Learns the demand signal without building the supply chain.

## Actionable vs Vanity Metrics

**Vanity metrics** (avoid): page views, registered users, total revenue — can grow while the business deteriorates.
**Actionable metrics** (use): cohort retention, activation rate, revenue per cohort, NPS — reveal causal mechanism.

**Three A's of metrics:** Actionable (demonstrate clear cause/effect), Accessible (simple, team-readable), Auditable (can verify data generation mechanism).

**Cohort analysis:** compare behavior of user groups by acquisition date, not aggregate. Distinguishes whether new features improved existing users or just attracted different users.

## The Pivot

A structured course correction to test a new fundamental hypothesis about product, strategy, and engine of growth. Not failure — failure is continuing to execute a plan that evidence has invalidated.

**Pivot types:**
- **Zoom-in** — narrow focus to one feature that becomes the whole product
- **Zoom-out** — single feature insufficient; expand to full product
- **Customer segment** — product works, but for different customer than assumed
- **Customer need** — customer problem is real, but not the one originally targeted
- **Platform** — single application → platform (or reverse)
- **Business architecture** — switch between high-margin/low-volume and low-margin/high-volume
- **Value capture** — change monetization model
- **Engine of growth** — switch from viral to sticky to paid
- **Channel** — change distribution mechanism
- **Technology** — achieve same solution via different technology

**Pivot or persevere meeting:** scheduled, not reactive. Prevents pivot-from-panic and persevere-from-denial.

## Engines of Growth

Three sustainable growth mechanisms — companies have one primary:

1. **Sticky** — high retention; growth comes from retention > churn rate. Metric: churn rate. If retention is high, need only small acquisition.
2. **Viral** — growth embedded in product use; each user recruits others. Metric: viral coefficient (>1 = exponential). Cost of acquisition → $0.
3. **Paid** — growth via paid acquisition; sustainable when LTV > CAC. Metric: LTV/CAC ratio. Must improve margins to accelerate.

Engines are exhausted over time. "Working the engine" = optimizing the current engine. But eventually growth requires finding a new one.

## Five Whys

Root cause analysis tool adapted for organizational learning. Ask "Why?" five times iteratively to trace a symptom to its systemic cause. Each level reveals an investment to prevent recurrence:

- System failed → Why? → Process broke → Why? → Engineer didn't know procedure → Why? → Not trained → Why? → No training budget → Why? → Leadership never prioritized it

**Proportional investment:** fix the root cause proportionally to severity. Not a blame tool — a process improvement tool.

## Innovation Accounting

Three milestones:
1. Establish a baseline using an MVP — measure where you are
2. Tune the engine — improve metrics from baseline toward ideal
3. Pivot or persevere — if metrics aren't improving after genuine effort, pivot

**Leap-of-faith assumptions:** the two most critical are the **value hypothesis** (does the product deliver value when used?) and the **growth hypothesis** (will growth happen and how?). Test these first.

## Large Company Application

**Internal entrepreneurship:** new products in large companies fail because they're subject to accountability standards appropriate for established businesses — revenue, margin, scale — before they've validated the market. Requires:
- Separate P&L for innovation initiatives
- Actionable metrics per initiative, not aggregate
- Protection from full overhead burden in early stage

**Continuous deployment / Andon cord:** build/test/deploy pipeline that catches defects as they're introduced. Any team member can halt deployment when anomaly detected — same as Toyota production system.
