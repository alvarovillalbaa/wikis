# Zero to One

[Source: WORK/BOOKS/Zero to One.pdf]

**Central thesis:** The most valuable businesses create something new (0→1 vertical progress) rather than copying existing models (1→n horizontal progress). Competition destroys value; monopoly creates it.

## The Contrarian Question

"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" — the foundation for non-consensus bets. Successful startups require a secret: something true about the world that most don't see or disagree with.

## Competition vs Monopoly

**Competitive markets:** commoditized, zero economic profit, firms obsess over competitors, lie about differentiation.
**Monopolies:** create enormous value, capture large fraction of the value they create, lie about not being monopolies (to avoid regulatory scrutiny).

The world lies in both directions: non-monopolists pretend to have differentiation; monopolists pretend to have competition.

**Google example:** <1% of search → "search engine" market is competitive. But if redefined as global advertising market (~$500B) → tiny share. Framing determines whether you look like a monopoly.

## Four Monopoly Characteristics

1. **Proprietary technology** — at least 10× better than closest substitute (not 10% — that's incremental, ignorable)
2. **Network effects** — product more valuable with more users; must be valuable to the first user (paradox: start small enough that network effects kick in immediately)
3. **Economies of scale** — fixed costs amortized over growing volume; software is the extreme case
4. **Brand** — cannot be the only moat; must be built on substance

## Last Mover Advantage

First mover = tactical, not strategic. Last mover = the one who makes the last great development in a market and enjoys monopoly profits for decades. Durability > initial market share.

**7 Questions for Business Analysis:**
1. Engineering — can you create breakthrough technology (10×)?
2. Timing — is now the right moment to enter?
3. Monopoly — are you starting with a big share of a small market?
4. People — do you have the right team?
5. Distribution — do you have a way to deliver the product?
6. Durability — will your market position be defensible in 10–20 years?
7. Secret — have you identified a unique opportunity others don't see?

## Start Small and Monopolize

Perfect target market: small group of particular people, concentrated together, served by few or no competitors. *Large market = bad choice. Large market already served by competitors = worst choice.* "Getting 1% of a $100B market" is a red flag — at 1% share in a huge market, competitive pressure eliminates profit.

**Scaling sequence:** dominate a niche → expand to adjacent markets. Amazon started with books (uniform shape, easy to ship, tail-demand advantage) → CDs/videos → general store. eBay started with Beanie Baby obsessives → hobbyists → general marketplace. Sequencing markets correctly is underrated; it takes discipline to not jump prematurely.

**Don't disrupt.** "Disruption" as framing puts you in incumbent's frame — you see yourself as insurgent fighting old firms instead of creating something new. Creation is more important than displacement.

## Power Law

Venture returns are not Gaussian — power law distribution. A small number of companies vastly outperform all others combined. Implication: only invest in companies that could potentially return the whole fund. This logic applies inside companies too: a few decisions/people/products dominate outcomes.

**Distribution follows the same power law.** Most businesses get zero distribution channels to work. Poor sales, not bad product, is the most common cause of failure. If you get just one channel to work, you have a great business. Kitchen-sink approach (a bit of everything) = none work.

## Secrets

Every great company is built around a secret — an important truth that most disagree with. Two types:
- **Secrets of nature** — undiscovered facts about the physical world
- **Secrets about people** — things people don't know about themselves or don't admit

Source of secrets: look at what is forbidden, ignored, or taboo. "What do people not talk about because it's unpopular?"

## Distribution

Engineers underestimate sales because sales works best when hidden. All salespeople disguise what they do with euphemistic titles: "account executive" = ad sales; "business development" = customer sales; "investment banker" = company sales. Good sales is invisible.

**Distribution spectrum by deal size:**
- **Complex sales** (>$1M deals): close personal attention, CEO-led, 1–2 deals/year; 50–100% YoY growth; start small (reference customers before bigger deals). No "salespeople" — CEO is the salesperson.
- **Personal sales** ($10K–$100K): scalable sales team; start with users with the most acute problem in a specific segment, then expand within accounts → eventually enterprise.
- **Distribution dead zone** (~$1K): too expensive for personal sales, too low-volume for mass advertising; products in this range often fail not for lack of quality but for lack of viable channel.
- **Advertising/marketing**: works when CLV < cost of personal sales AND no viral path; only viable for mass-market products.
- **Viral**: core functionality encourages invitations. Requires fast loop. Target the highest-velocity users first (PayPal → eBay PowerSellers, not random eBay users).

Sell your company to employees and investors with the same rigor as selling your product. "Great company sells itself" is the HR version of "great product sells itself" — both are lies.

## Founding Dynamics

**Founding team:** co-founders must know each other well beforehand. Equity splits decided early and clearly. Avoid equal splits (avoids hard decisions).

**Three distinctions:**
- **Ownership** — who legally owns equity
- **Possession** — who runs the company day-to-day
- **Control** — who formally governs the company (board)

Misalignment of these three is a primary source of startup conflict.

**CEO pay as a signal:** low CEO salary (<$150K/yr) correlates with high performance. High salary creates risk aversion and misaligned incentives.

## Definite vs Indefinite Optimism

|  | Optimist | Pessimist |
|--|--|--|
| **Definite** | Plan → execute → build (US 1950–2000) | Plan to decline (Malthus, managed decay) |
| **Indefinite** | Hope without plan (US 2000–) | Diversify against inevitable decline (EU) |

Indefinite optimism is the dominant current mode: save, diversify, preserve optionality. But great founders are **definite optimists** — they have a specific vision and execute toward it.

## The Lean Startup Critique

Iteration and small experiments are appropriate when you don't know what to build. But iteration without a definite vision is just drift. "A business with a good definite plan will always be underrated in a world where people underestimate the power of conviction."

## Stagnation and Progress

Two types of progress: **horizontal/extensive** (1→n, globalization) and **vertical/intensive** (0→1, technology). Technology is the lever for doing more with less — the only path to escape Malthusian resource competition as the world converges on developed-country living standards.
