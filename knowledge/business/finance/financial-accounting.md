## What Is Accounting

- **Definition:** "The measurement, processing and communication of financial information about economic entities"
- Accounting is the language of business — it enables decision-makers inside and outside firms to make informed choices
- Financial accounting is distinct from managerial accounting: financial accounting produces statements for external stakeholders (investors, creditors, regulators)

### Users of Accounting Information

| User | Primary Information Need |
|---|---|
| **Shareholders / investors** | Future cash flows of the firm |
| **Creditors** | Liquidity, risk, and profitability |
| **Employees** | Firm stability and employment contract negotiation |
| **Customers** | Firm's ability to honor warranties |
| **Management** | Decision-making and compensation contracts |

## Three Core Financial Statements

Module 1 (Financial Accounting I) covers all three in sequence:

| Statement | What it shows |
|---|---|
| **Balance sheet** | Snapshot of assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time |
| **Income statement** | Revenues and expenses over a period → net income |
| **Cash flow statement** | Cash inflows and outflows over a period; reconciles income to cash |

### Fundamental Accounting Equation

```
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
```

- Holds at all times; every transaction must preserve this balance
- For any period: `Δ Assets = Δ Liabilities + Δ Shareholders' Equity`
- This is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping

### Balance Sheet Components

**Module 1 lesson structure:**
- Assets (Lesson 1-2.2): current assets (cash, receivables, inventory) + non-current assets (PP&E, intangibles)
- Liabilities (Lesson 1-2.3): current (payable within 1 year) + long-term (bonds, leases, deferred taxes)
- Shareholders' equity (Lesson 1-2.4): common stock + retained earnings + AOCI

## Recording Financial Transactions

**Three-step process (from Module 2):**
1. Identify which accounts are impacted by the transaction
2. Determine the direction and amount of impact on each account
3. Ensure the fundamental accounting equation holds after recording

**Transaction worksheet format:**

| | Assets | = | Liabilities | + | Shareholders' Equity |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Opening balance | | | | | |
| Transactions | | | | | |
| Closing balance | | | | | |

**Example:** Firm purchases a truck for $65,000 with a bank loan
- Fixed assets (Assets) +$65,000
- Bank loan (Liabilities) +$65,000
- Shareholders' equity: unchanged
- Equation check: ΔAssets ($65K) = ΔLiabilities ($65K) + ΔEquity ($0) ✓

## Accrual Accounting (Module 2)

Covered in Lesson 2-4 (TOC only; content at page 27):
- **Accrual basis:** revenues recognized when earned, expenses recognized when incurred — regardless of cash movement
- Contrast with **cash basis:** recognize only when cash changes hands
- GAAP and IFRS require accrual accounting for external financial reporting
- Creates timing differences that explain why net income ≠ cash flow from operations

## Long-Term Assets and Depreciation (Financial Accounting II, Module 1)

### Asset Recognition Criteria

An expenditure is capitalized as a balance sheet asset only if it satisfies **both** criteria:
1. **Benefit is quantifiable** — the economic benefit from the expenditure can be reliably measured
2. **Rights to use are obtained via past transactions** — legal or contractual right exists

**Practical classification examples:**

| Expenditure | Asset or Expense? | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Buildings | Asset (tangible) | Quantifiable value; acquired via contract |
| Land | Asset (tangible) | Quantifiable value; acquired via contract |
| R&D expenditures | Expense | Benefit not quantifiable (may yield nothing) |
| Advertising | Expense | Benefit not quantifiable |
| Training | Expense | Benefit not quantifiable |
| Patent purchased | Asset (intangible) | Quantifiable (purchase price is known); rights obtained |

- **R&D and advertising are always expensed** under US GAAP — even though they may create long-term value, the benefit fails the quantifiability test
- **Internally developed patents** are expensed (benefit uncertain); **purchased patents** are capitalized (purchase price is a reliable measure)
- Tangible assets: physically touchable (buildings, equipment, land)
- Intangible assets: no physical form but appear on balance sheet (patents, trademarks, goodwill)

## FA I Module 3 — Fundamental Accounting Concepts + Accruals + Revenue Recognition

[Source: WORK/KNOWLEDGE/Business/Financial Management/Financial Accounting I/Module 3.pdf]

### Five Accounting Principles

1. **Going concern:** assume firm operates indefinitely — otherwise balance sheet values are misleading (assets overstated, liabilities understated in bankruptcy)
2. **Conservatism:** book revenues only when earned; book expenses even under uncertainty → asymmetric prudence
3. **Matching principle:** recognize costs in the same period as the revenues they generated
4. **Consistency:** same rules across periods (temporal) and across firms (cross-sectional) — enables comparison
5. **Accruals principle:** recognize revenues when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of cash movement

### Accruals vs. Deferrals

- **Accrual:** economic event first, cash later
  - Accrued revenue: service performed → debit A/R, credit Revenue; cash received later closes A/R
  - Accrued expense: expense incurred → debit Expense, credit A/P; cash paid later closes A/P
- **Deferral:** cash first, economic event later
  - Prepaid expense (e.g., prepaid rent): cash paid → debit Prepaid Asset; expense recognized as asset is consumed
  - Unearned revenue (e.g., advance payment): cash received → credit Unearned Revenue (liability); revenue recognized as service delivered

### Revenue Recognition (GAAP)

- Revenue recognized when **earned** (service performed / goods delivered), not when cash received
- Year-end closing: income statement balances transferred to Retained Earnings

## FA I Module 4 — Short-Term Assets: Accounts Receivable and Inventories

[Source: WORK/KNOWLEDGE/Business/Financial Management/Financial Accounting I/Module 4.pdf]

### Accounts Receivable

- Valued at **net realizable value (NRV):** face value − cash discounts − sales returns − bad debts
- **Cash discounts:** e.g., "3/15 n/30" = 3% discount if paid within 15 days, full amount due within 30 days; discount recorded as reduction from sales (contra-revenue)
- **Sales return allowance:** contra-asset; estimated before returns occur (matching principle); debit Sales Return Allowance, credit A/R when return materializes
- **Bad debts — allowance method (GAAP required):**
  - Estimate period's uncollectibles → debit Bad Debt Expense, credit Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (contra-asset)
  - Write-off when confirmed uncollectible: debit Allowance, credit A/R — no P&L impact at write-off
  - **Aging method:** apply probability-of-default rates by bucket (0–30 days, 31–60 days, 61–90 days, >90 days)

### Inventories

- Current asset; cost flows: Balance Sheet → Income Statement (COGS) when sold
- **FIFO (First In, First Out):** oldest costs to COGS; inventory at recent prices; higher profit in rising-price environments
- **LIFO (Last In, First Out):** newest costs to COGS; lower taxable income when prices rise; **not permitted under IFRS**
- **Weighted average:** blended cost per unit applied uniformly; smooths price fluctuations
- LIFO reserve = FIFO inventory − LIFO inventory; discloses real inventory value to analysts
- **Lower of cost or net realizable value (LCM/NRV):** if market drops below cost, write down to market (conservatism)

## FA II Module 2 — Liabilities

[Source: WORK/KNOWLEDGE/Business/Financial Management/Financial Accounting II/Module 2.pdf]

### Recognition Criteria

A liability requires: (1) **measurable** (quantifiable value), and (2) **from a past executed contract** (obligation already incurred)

### Short-Term Liabilities (≤ 1 year): valued at face value

- A/P, wages payable, utility payable, unearned revenue (delivered → revenue), current portion of long-term debt

### Long-Term Liabilities (> 1 year): valued at present value of future obligations

- **Bonds payable:**
  - Issued at par (coupon = market rate): proceeds = face value
  - Issued at discount (coupon < market rate): proceeds < face; discount amortized → increases effective interest expense over life
  - Issued at premium (coupon > market rate): proceeds > face; premium amortized → reduces effective interest expense
  - **Carrying value** = face ± unamortized premium/discount
- **Leases (post-ASC 842 / IFRS 16):** nearly all leases now on balance sheet; right-of-use asset + lease liability; finance lease vs. operating lease distinction (different P&L presentation)

## FA II Module 3 — Shareholders' Equity

[Source: WORK/KNOWLEDGE/Business/Financial Management/Financial Accounting II/Module 3.pdf]

- **SE = Assets − Liabilities** by fundamental equation
- **Key invariant:** transactions between firm and shareholders never create profit or loss for the firm
- Components: Common Stock (at par) + Capital in Excess of Par + Retained Earnings − Treasury Stock

### Three Drivers of SE Change

1. **Equity issuance:** Cash↑, Common Stock (par)↑, APIC↑ (excess above par value)
   - **Par value:** historical nominal artifact (e.g., $0.01/share); no economic significance — total issue price matters
2. **Profitability:** Net Income → Retained Earnings↑; Net Loss → RE↓
3. **Distributions:**
   - **Cash dividend:** Declaration → Dividends Payable (liability); Payment → settles liability + reduces RE
   - **Stock dividend:** reclassify from RE to paid-in capital; total SE unchanged; dilutes per-share value
   - **Treasury stock:** buyback reduces SE (debit Treasury Stock, contra-equity); resale: proceeds above cost → APIC; proceeds below → reduce APIC then RE

### Earnings Per Share

- **Basic EPS** = Net Income / Weighted Average Shares Outstanding
- **Diluted EPS** = Net Income / (Weighted Avg Shares + dilutive securities: options, warrants, convertibles)
- Diluted ≤ Basic always; large gap signals option/convertible overhang

## FA II Module 4 — Cash Flow Statement

[Source: WORK/KNOWLEDGE/Business/Financial Management/Financial Accounting II/Module 4.pdf]

### Net Income ≠ Cash Flow

- Accrual accounting decouples revenue/expense recognition from cash timing
- A firm can be highly profitable and cash-poor (sells on long credit terms, prepays suppliers)
- Cash flow statement provides liquidity picture income statement omits

### Three Sections

| Section | Content | Typical sign |
|---|---|---|
| **CFO (Operating)** | Cash from core operations: customer receipts − supplier/employee payments − taxes | Positive and growing = healthy |
| **CFI (Investing)** | Long-term investment cash: capex, acquisitions, asset disposals | Usually negative (growing firms invest) |
| **CFF (Financing)** | Capital structure cash flows: equity issuance/buybacks, debt borrowing/repayment, dividends paid | Varies by lifecycle stage |

`Ending cash = Beginning cash + ΔCFO + ΔCFI + ΔCFF`

### Indirect Method (CFO — most common presentation)

Start with Net Income, then adjust:
1. **Add back** non-cash charges: depreciation, amortization, impairments
2. **Working capital adjustments:**
   - ↑A/R → subtract (revenue recognized, cash not yet received)
   - ↑Inventory → subtract (cash spent, not yet expensed)
   - ↑A/P → add (expense recognized, cash not yet paid)
   - ↑Unearned revenue → add (cash received, not yet earned)

### Earnings Quality

- Net Income >> CFO persistently → aggressive accruals → earnings quality risk
- Negative CFO with positive net income = red flag; common in pre-revenue growth companies
- Walmart (2015): ending cash $9,135M; CFO large positive (retail operations); CFI negative (store build-out); CFF negative (dividends + buybacks)
