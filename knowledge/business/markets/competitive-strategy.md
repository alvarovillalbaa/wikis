# Competitive Strategy

[Source: WORK/BOOKS/Michael-E.-Porter-Competitive-Strategy.pdf — Porter, 1980, 400pp. Supplemented from full-text extraction: Chs 3, 5, 9–13]

**Central thesis:** Strategy is the deliberate choice of a competitive position that allows a firm to earn above-average returns. Position is defended by structural forces — Five Forces — not by operational effectiveness alone.

## Five Forces Framework

Industry attractiveness (long-run profitability potential) is determined by five structural forces:

### 1. Threat of New Entrants
Moderated by entry barriers:
- **Economies of scale** — per-unit cost advantage that incumbents have; entrant must enter at scale or accept cost disadvantage
- **Product differentiation** — brand loyalty as switching barrier; new entrant must spend to overcome it
- **Capital requirements** — especially where capital is sunk (advertising, R&D) rather than collateralizable
- **Switching costs** — one-time costs buyer faces when switching supplier
- **Access to distribution channels** — shelf space, dealer networks — entrant must displace or create
- **Cost advantages independent of scale** — proprietary technology, learning curve, favorable locations, government subsidies, pre-Internet access to raw materials
- **Government policy** — licensing, regulatory requirements, environmental standards

Entry is a threat even if it doesn't occur — the *potential* constrains incumbent pricing.

### 2. Intensity of Rivalry Among Existing Competitors
Determinants: number of competitors, industry growth rate, fixed/storage costs, lack of differentiation, capacity augmented in large increments, exit barriers, diversity of competitors, strategic stakes.

**Exit barriers** are the underappreciated rival of entry barriers: high exit barriers (specialized assets, emotional commitment, government/social restrictions) trap capacity in declining industries → sustained price wars.

### 3. Pressure from Substitute Products
Products in other industries that perform the same function. Substitutes set a ceiling on industry prices. Most dangerous substitutes are those: (a) improving price-performance vs. the industry, (b) produced by high-profit industries.

### 4. Bargaining Power of Buyers
Buyers are powerful when: concentrated or buy in large volume; product is standardized (no lock-in); low switching costs; buyer earns low profits (strong incentive to extract price); buyer credibly threatens backward integration; product is not important to buyer's quality.

**Buyer selection strategy:** choose buyers with low power and high switching costs. Pricing power is partly a function of *which* customers you serve.

### 5. Bargaining Power of Suppliers
Suppliers powerful when: supplier group dominated by few companies; no substitute inputs; industry is not an important customer for supplier group; supplier product is an important input to buyer's business; supplier products differentiated or have switching costs; supplier credibly threatens forward integration.

## Three Generic Strategies

A firm must choose a distinct competitive position; attempting to occupy multiple simultaneously results in "stuck in the middle" — suboptimal returns from all positions.

### 1. Cost Leadership
- Single low-cost position in the industry
- Requires: aggressive capital investment, value engineering, tight cost control, learning curve exploitation, standardized products
- Protects against: all five forces (rivals can't compete on price; buyers can only push price to the next-most-efficient competitor's level; supplier power absorbed through margins; substitutes less attractive; entry barriers from scale/experience)
- Risk: technology change that obsoletes the cost basis; competitors learning the cost leader's methods; demand shift toward differentiated offerings

### 2. Differentiation
- Uniqueness on dimensions buyers value: brand, technology, customer service, dealer network, features
- Does not ignore cost — just not the *primary* target
- Protects against: buyer power (reduced sensitivity to price); supplier power (absorbed through margins); entry barriers (via brand loyalty); substitute pressure (uniqueness makes cross-industry switching harder)
- Risk: cost differential between differentiated firm and cost leader becomes too large; buyers' need for differentiating factor diminishes; imitation reduces perceived uniqueness

### 3. Focus
- Serve a narrow target segment better than broad-scope competitors
- Two variants: **cost focus** (cost advantage in the target segment) vs **differentiation focus** (differentiation within the segment)
- Logic: target segment must have different buyer needs than the broader industry, OR the cost structure serving it must differ
- Risk: the target segment's differences from the broader market erode; broad-scope competitors focus down into your segment; segment becomes too small

## Stuck in the Middle

Firms without a clear position earn below-average returns. Attempting all three = mediocre at all three. The implicit strategy of many companies — compete on a mix of cost, differentiation, and focus without discipline — is the most common cause of strategic failure.

## Strategic Groups

Clusters of firms within an industry following the same or similar strategy. Mobility barriers = entry barriers specific to a strategic group (not just the industry). Firms within a strategic group are closer competitors than firms across group boundaries.

**Mapping strategic groups:** two dimensions (chosen to explain maximum variance in strategy) → plot firms → groups visible. Proximity on map = rivalry intensity.

## Industry Evolution

Industries follow predictable structural trajectories: fragmented early-stage → shakeout → mature oligopoly → potential decline. Generic strategy must match the structural phase:

- **Emerging:** uncertainty about product form, technology, competitors; key = establishing position before structure solidifies
- **Transition to maturity:** slowing growth triggers strategic reorientation; cost and service replace growth as competitive levers; buyer sophistication increases
- **Declining:** absolute shrinkage; four strategic postures — leadership (dominant share), niche, harvest, divest. Choose based on: whether the decline is inevitable, structure of residual demand pockets, own competitive strengths

## Competitive Signals

Observable market behaviors that indicate intent or reaction:
- **Prior announcements** — signal future moves to deter competitive response
- **Post-announcement** — communicate satisfaction/dissatisfaction to calibrate rivals' models
- **Cross-parry** — responding to a rival's move in one market by attacking their home market

Distinguish between **bluffs** (signals not backed by commitment) and **credible signals** (backed by irreversible commitments — capacity builds, long-term contracts, visible investment).

## Vertical Integration

Forward (toward buyer) or backward (toward supplier) integration. Decision criteria:
- **Market power** — integration can foreclose rivals from supply or distribution
- **Economies of combined operations** — only relevant when transactions require asset specificity
- **Strategic analysis:** integration makes sense when it improves the firm's position against at least one of the five forces more than the alternative governance modes

Transaction cost logic: integrate when the cost of market governance (price search, contracting, enforcement) exceeds the cost of hierarchy.

## Competitor Analysis Framework

Four components forming a **response profile** — predicting how a competitor will behave, not just what it can do:

### What Drives the Competitor (hidden, high-predictive-value)
1. **Future goals** — financial targets, growth aspirations, attitudes toward risk, organizational incentives, parent company objectives. Determine satisfaction with current position → whether the competitor will initiate strategic change.
2. **Assumptions** — what the competitor believes about itself ("we are the quality leader") and about the industry ("price competition is destructive"). Blind spots arise here. Assumptions about cost position, technology, and buyer behavior shape every decision; erroneous assumptions create exploitable vulnerabilities.

### What the Competitor Is Doing and Can Do (visible, diagnostic)
3. **Current strategy** — how the competitor is actually competing (product positioning, pricing, channel, marketing).
4. **Capabilities** — strengths and weaknesses: relative cost position, financial resources, product quality, manufacturing, distribution, R&D, management depth.

**Response profile** from combining all four:
- *Offensive moves*: Is the competitor satisfied with its current position? What probable moves will it make? How strong and serious?
- *Defensive capability*: What moves make the competitor most vulnerable? What triggers disproportionate retaliation (*hot buttons*)? To what moves can it not respond effectively given its goals, capabilities, and assumptions?
- *Battleground selection*: Find moves competitors are frozen from matching given their current strategy's internal commitments, goals, and assumptions.

## Competitive Moves

### Industry Instability Prerequisites
Warfare likelihood is structural: high competitor count, equal power distribution, undifferentiated products, high fixed costs, slow growth → higher instability. Diversity of goals and strategic stakes → harder to interpret signals → less stable outcomes. *History of interaction and multiple arenas of competition promote stability*.

### Cooperative / Nonthreatening Moves
Three categories ranked by risk:
1. Moves that improve the firm's position **and** improve competitors' positions even if they don't match — lowest risk; often corrections of industry-damaging practices.
2. Moves that improve positions **only if** a significant number of competitors follow — risk is defection; assess each competitor's incentive to break ranks.
3. Moves that improve position **because competitors won't follow** — depends on deep competitor analysis: competitors don't perceive it as competition (Timex entering watches via drugstores while Swiss focused on jewelers; Swiss saw Timex as a "junk product," not competition).

Signal nonthreatening intent explicitly (press, announcements, pricing tied to visible indices). Rely on recognized industry leaders to move first.

### Threatening Moves — Predicting Retaliation
Five questions for offensive moves: (1) likelihood of retaliation, (2) speed, (3) effectiveness, (4) toughness (willingness to retaliate at own expense), (5) can retaliation be influenced?

**Perceptual lags** — move quietly, in peripheral markets or away from competitors' monitoring systems. Competitors dependent on trade association data can be hours or quarters behind. Diversionary tactics (visible move elsewhere) extend lags.

**Execution lags** — choose move types where competitors face the longest retaliatory path: price cuts can be matched overnight; new plants take 3–5 years; new automobile models take 3 years.

### Commitment
Commitment = communicating intentions unequivocally to cause competitors to recalculate their rational strategies from new assumptions.

**Three types:**
1. Commitment to stick with a move → deters retaliation (competitors accept new position rather than fight)
2. Commitment to retaliate to rivals' moves → deters initiation (credible threat of bitter retaliation stops the move before it starts)
3. Commitment to not act → builds trust, deescalates warfare (firm takes demonstrable performance hit that benefits rivals as proof)

**Communicating commitment credibly requires:**
- Assets/resources to carry out the commitment quickly (excess capacity, cash, fighting brands, shelf products ready to deploy against a specific competitor)
- History of adherence to past commitments
- Irreversible actions — buy vs. lease, public contracts, market share targets announced to the financial community
- Perceived moral/organizational resolve to not back down (even bordering on irrationality)
- Detection ability — competitors must believe violations will be caught immediately (known monitoring systems, field sales force, distributor networks)

**First-mover advantage in commitment** — the firm that commits first forces competitors to take its behavior as given. Credibility compounds over time; undoing a commitment costs more than the original cost of making it.

## Generic Industry Environments (Part II)

### Fragmented Industries
No single firm holds significant market share; large numbers of small/medium competitors. Causes:
- Low entry barriers (necessary but insufficient alone)
- Absent economies of scale or experience curve (labor-intensive, personal service, customization, local supervision — lobster fishing, restaurants, consulting)
- High transportation costs relative to value → limits efficient plant radius (cement, fluid milk)
- High inventory costs or erratic sales → penalizes large-scale fixed facilities
- No size advantage in buyer/supplier bargaining
- Diseconomies of scale for quality/creative output, local image, close personal supervision, frequent style changes

**Strategic options in fragmented industries:**
- *Overcoming fragmentation* — standardize and scale what competitors treat as inherently local (Marriott, McDonald's in restaurant/hotel); create proprietary methods, centralized buying, process automation
- *Tightly managed decentralization* — franchise model; central brand/systems with local owner-operator accountability
- *Specialization* — dominate narrow product type or customer segment before broader competitors notice
- *Niche in a pocket of low exit barriers* — wait for industry consolidation and position there

### Emerging Industries
**No rules of the game.** Structural characteristics: technological uncertainty (no dominant design), strategic uncertainty, high initial costs declining steeply along the learning curve, embryonic companies and spin-offs (low barriers enable entry; equity stakes attract talent from incumbents).

**Key strategic issues:**
- Shaping industry standards and dominant design before they solidify — early positions are cheaper to establish than later challenges
- Early adopter selection — buyers who are predisposed to unproven technology, have autonomous budget authority, and are reachable determine initial positioning and referenceability
- Manage the uncertainty asymmetry: firms that commit early to a technology/standard that wins gain large advantages; premature commitment to a losing standard can be fatal
- Beware of overshooting — building scale for a market that hasn't materialized yet; balance entry cost against entry timing

### Declining Industries
Absolute sustained unit decline — not cyclical. The received wisdom (harvest, then divest) is frequently suboptimal.

**Structural determinants of decline intensity:** pace of demand decline; certainty/uncertainty about whether decline is permanent (uncertainty → firms hold on → warfare); demand pocket structure (some niches may be stable while overall industry declines); nature of exit barriers (sunk assets, labor agreements, government/social pressure → capacity stays in despite poor returns).

**Four end-game strategies:**
1. **Leadership** — invest to become the dominant survivor; buy out competitors; exploit the shrinking demand for above-average returns through scale
2. **Niche** — identify a pocket of stable or slowly declining demand and dominate it; protect from over-investment as the broader industry collapses
3. **Harvest** — eliminate new investment, maximize cash flow, manage orderly contraction
4. **Divest early** — exit before decline is fully recognized by the market; capture highest residual value before assets are stigmatized

Choice depends on: the firm's competitive position within the declining industry, the structure of residual demand pockets, the firm's exit barriers, and whether the decline is reversible.

### Global Industries
A global industry is one where competitive position in *each* national market is fundamentally affected by the firm's overall *worldwide* position. Distinction: multinational ≠ global. A multinational with independent local subsidiaries (consumer packaged food) competes country-by-country; a global competitor (IBM, auto, motorcycles) derives advantages from coordinated worldwide operations.

**Sources of global competition:**
- Comparative advantage in factor costs (labor, materials) → low-cost country export platforms
- Scale economies achievable only at global volume → forces cross-border production concentration
- Learning curve across global volume → cost advantage compound
- Differentiation built on global R&D, technology, or brand

**Impediments to global competition (and potential niches):** differing product standards, buyer needs, and distribution structures; government restrictions; cultural barriers; high transportation relative to product value; service and delivery requirements favoring local production.

**Strategic options:** pure global (one integrated worldwide strategy), protected markets strategy (exploit regulatory barriers), national focus in specific pockets where global advantages are lowest and local differentiation highest.
