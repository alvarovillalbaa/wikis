## Marketing Fundamentals (tema1)

### Need → Desire → Demand Chain

- **Need** — state of perceived lack (physical, social, individual); need is not created by marketing
- **Desire** — specific form a need takes when shaped by culture, personality, and experience; marketing shapes desires
- **Demand** — desire backed by purchasing power; the only economically actionable unit

### Market Definition

- Market = total potential buyers for a product/service category
- Market size drives investment decisions; underdefining a market = missed opportunity; overdefining = wasted resources

### Four Company Orientations

| Orientation | Focus | Assumption | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Production** | Efficiency, low cost, wide distribution | Consumers buy cheap/available products | Ignores quality gaps |
| **Product** | Quality, innovation, features | Consumers pay for best product | Marketing myopia (better mousetrap trap) |
| **Sales** | Aggressive promotion and distribution | Consumers buy unless pushed | Short-term, creates mistrust |
| **Marketing** | Customer needs first, then produce | Profit through customer satisfaction | Requires deep market research |

- Evolution is not strictly linear — companies can hold mixed orientations
- "Marketing myopia" (Levitt): product-oriented firms define their business too narrowly and miss market shifts

### Strategic Marketing Framework (4 Questions)

1. **Mission** — what business are we in? (broad enough to pivot, narrow enough to focus)
2. **Environment** — external opportunities and threats; internal strengths and weaknesses
3. **Objectives** — measurable targets derived from mission and environmental scan
4. **Strategy** — how to reach objectives given constraints (marketing mix, segmentation decisions)

### Digital Marketing Types

- **SEO** (Search Engine Optimization) — organic traffic through content and technical structure
- **SEM** (Search Engine Marketing) — paid search advertising (Google Ads, etc.)
- **SMO** (Social Media Optimization) — building brand presence and engagement on social platforms
- **Content marketing** — pull strategy; attract through value-generating content
- **Email marketing** — direct channel; high ROI when list is permission-based
- **Affiliate marketing** — performance-based partnerships; pay on conversion

## Segmentation and Positioning (tema2)

### Market Segmentation

- Segmentation = dividing a heterogeneous market into homogeneous sub-groups with distinct needs, responses, or behaviors
- Purpose: enables targeted value propositions and efficient resource allocation

### Four Levels of Segmentation

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Segments** | Large identifiable groups within a market (demographic, geographic, psychographic, behavioral) |
| **Niches** | Smaller, more narrowly defined sub-segments; often underserved, willing to pay premium |
| **Local markets** | Geographic concentration; enables hyper-local offers and messaging |
| **One-to-one** | Individualized marketing; enabled by data and personalization technology |

### Three Target Strategies

- **Undifferentiated (mass)** — single offer to whole market; efficient but ignores heterogeneity
- **Differentiated** — separate offers for multiple segments; higher costs, higher potential revenue
- **Concentrated (niche)** — deep focus on one segment; lower cost, higher expertise, higher segment-level risk

### Positioning

- Positioning = the mental image the target customer holds about the brand relative to competitors
- It is a perception problem, not a product problem — positioning happens in the customer's mind
- Differentiation is the mechanism for achieving desired positioning

**Differentiation strategy (3 steps):**
1. Identify meaningful differences the company can deliver (product, service, people, channel, image)
2. Select differences that are important to target segment, deliverable, and defensible
3. Communicate and deliver the selected differences consistently

**Positioning map:**
- 2-axis perceptual map plotting brand positions on key dimensions (e.g., price vs. quality)
- Identifies gaps (unoccupied positions) and crowding (competitive clusters)
- Useful for detecting repositioning opportunities

## Marketing Information Systems and Client Analysis (tema3)

### Data → Information → Knowledge Chain

- **Data** — raw facts; no inherent meaning without context
- **Information** — data processed and interpreted within a context
- **Knowledge** — information integrated with experience and judgment; actionable

### SIM — Marketing Information System

Three component sources (must be integrated, not siloed):

| Source | Description |
|---|---|
| **Internal data** | Sales records, CRM data, order history, service logs — high volume, readily available, often under-utilized |
| **Marketing intelligence** | Continuous environmental scan — competitor monitoring, trade press, retail feedback, supplier data |
| **Marketing research** | Commissioned studies; primary (surveys, interviews, observation) or secondary (published reports) |

- Occasional data systems: commissioned for specific decisions (one-off research)
- Preferential data systems: continuous monitoring with persistent data store

### Big Data Caveat

- More data ≠ better marketing decisions — requires behavioral experts to interpret
- Common failure: firms collect large datasets but lack analytical capability to extract actionable patterns
- Data without hypothesis is noise; effective marketing analytics starts with a business question

### Internal vs. External Data

- **Internal** — transactional, behavioral, operational; company-controlled; typically structured
- **External** — market, economic, competitive, social; sourced from third parties; often unstructured

## Web Analytics and Metrics (tema4)

### Web Analytics Cycle

plan → implement → measure → interpret → optimize → decide → (loop)

- Measurement without interpretation is useless; interpretation without action is waste
- Cycle must be institutionalized as a recurring process, not a one-time audit

### Five Core Web Metrics

| Metric | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Unique visitors** | Distinct individuals visiting the site within a period; proxy for audience reach |
| **Visits / Sessions** | Number of individual browsing sessions; one visitor can have multiple sessions |
| **Pageviews** | Total pages loaded; indicates content depth engagement |
| **Avg. session duration** | Mean time spent per visit; proxy for engagement quality |
| **Bounce rate** | % sessions with single-page view and no interaction; high bounce = relevance/UX failure |

### Campaign Performance Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **CPM** | Cost per 1,000 impressions | Awareness pricing |
| **CPC** | Cost per click | Traffic acquisition cost |
| **CPL** | Cost per lead | Lead generation efficiency |
| **CPA** | Cost per acquisition | Conversion efficiency |
| **Revenue Sharing** | % of revenue from referred sale | Affiliate/partnership model |

### Efficacy Chain

CPM → CTR (click-through rate) → LTR (lead-through rate) → TC (transaction/conversion) → RS (revenue share)

- Each step is a conversion gate; optimize the weakest link first
- CTR = clicks / impressions; low CTR = creative/targeting problem
- LTR = leads / clicks; low LTR = landing page or offer mismatch

### Analytics Methods

- **Qualitative** — heatmaps, session recordings, usability testing; reveals *why* users behave as they do
- **Quantitative** — statistical aggregation of behavioral data; reveals *what* users do and *how often*
- **Logs-based** — server-side tracking; captures all traffic including bots; no JS dependency
- **Tags-based** (pixel/JS) — client-side; more accurate for user behavior; blocked by ad-blockers
- **Census tools** — measure full population (e.g., Google Analytics); high volume, variable quality
- **Panel tools** — measure representative sample (e.g., comScore, Nielsen); lower volume, normalized

## Relational Marketing and CRM (tema5)

### Kotler's 4-Step Customer Relationship Process

1. **Identify** profitable customer segments worth investing in
2. **Differentiate** offering by customer value and needs
3. **Interact** with customers across touchpoints to deepen relationship
4. **Personalize** products, services, and communications at the individual level

### Five Levels of Relational Marketing

| Level | Description |
|---|---|
| **Basic** | Sell the product; no follow-up |
| **Reactive** | Respond if customer contacts you |
| **Statistical** | Monitor customer data and send relevant outreach |
| **Proactive** | Anticipate needs and reach out before customer asks |
| **Partner** | Full co-creation; brand and customer evolve together |

- Higher margin products → higher investment in proactive/partner relationships
- Commodity products → statistical or reactive sufficient

### Traditional vs. 1-to-1 Marketing

| Dimension | Traditional | 1-to-1 |
|---|---|---|
| Target | Average customer, mass segment | Individual customer |
| Message | Uniform | Personalized |
| Channel | Mass media | Direct, data-driven |
| Goal | Market share | Share of customer (wallet) |
| Metric | Transactions | Lifetime value (LTV) |
| Relationship | Transactional | Continuous |

### CRM — Customer Relationship Management

- CRM is a **strategy**, not software — software is only the enabler (Kotler, Thompson, Piphanyk)
- Core purpose: identify high-value customers, deepen relationships, reduce churn
- Benefits (Peppers & Rogers):
  - Loyalty increase → reduced acquisition cost
  - Reduced discount dependency (loyal customers less price-sensitive)
  - Optimized CAC through better targeting
  - Up to **42% benefit improvement** from mature CRM practice
- CRM failure modes: deploying CRM software without cultural/process change; treating all customers equally
