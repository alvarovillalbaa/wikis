# Behavioral Economics

[Source: WORK/BOOKS/Predictably Irrational.epub]

**Central thesis:** Human decision-making is systematically irrational in predictable, repeatable ways. Standard economics assumes rational actors maximizing utility; behavioral economics catalogs the structural deviations from that model and their implications for pricing, persuasion, and policy.

## Anchoring

The first number in a negotiation or evaluation context becomes an anchor that disproportionately influences subsequent judgment, even when the anchor is arbitrary or known to be random.

**Mechanism:** adjustment from an anchor is insufficient; people stop adjusting when they reach a "plausible" value, which remains close to the anchor.

**Implications:** 
- Salary negotiation: state a high first number → anchors the counterparty's adjustment
- Product pricing: anchor with a high price before the actual price
- Ariely's experiment: SSN digits used as starting bids → strong correlation between SSN and willingness-to-pay for unrelated items

**Self-herding:** your own past behavior becomes an anchor. If you paid $X for coffee last week, you're likely to pay $X again — not because $X is fair but because it's consistent with your prior behavior. The initial price anchored itself.

## Arbitrary Coherence

Preferences are not pre-formed; they are constructed in the moment of decision. The first evaluation of a new category creates a preference that persists and shapes future evaluations. The initial anchor is arbitrary; the coherence with subsequent choices is real.

**Experiment:** subjects wrote last two digits of SSN, then bid on items. High-SSN groups bid 60–120% more than low-SSN groups on identical items. Randomness created the anchor; coherent behavior followed.

## FREE! Effect

Zero-price items are cognitively overvalued — the zero price triggers a qualitatively different response than any positive price. The jump from $0.01 to $0.00 is larger than the jump from $0.15 to $0.14.

**Mechanism:** every transaction has potential downside. At price > $0, this potential loss must be evaluated. At $0, no downside exists → no evaluation cost → unconditional positive response.

**Amazon Prime example:** free shipping offer drives disproportionate behavior — people add items to orders to "qualify" for free shipping even when shipping cost < the added item cost.

## Market vs Social Norms

Two distinct cognitive frames that govern exchange:

**Market norms:** prices, wages, cost-benefit, explicit quid pro quo. Operating under market norms → rational evaluation of offers.

**Social norms:** gifts, favors, community, relationship reciprocity. No direct exchange calculation. Higher engagement, more flexibility, more intrinsic motivation.

**The conflict:** introducing market norms into a social norm context destroys the social norm and replaces it with a lower-value market transaction. It is nearly impossible to reverse.

**ACLU example (Ariely):** lawyers asked to work for low rate → rejected. Same lawyers asked to do the same work pro bono → accepted. Small payment invoked market norms; no payment left social norms in place.

**Implications for employment:** companies that pay social wages (bonuses, perks, benefits, status) get social-norm engagement levels. Companies that cut bonuses, add tracking, convert everything to transactional terms get market-norm engagement. The switch is asymmetric — easy to shift into market norms, hard to recover social norms.

## The Endowment Effect (Ownership Bias)

Once you own something, you value it more than you would to acquire it. Loss aversion (Kahneman/Tversky): losses are approximately 2× more painful than equivalent gains.

**Mechanism:** ownership shifts the reference point. Selling means giving something up (a loss); not buying means forgoing a gain. Losses hurt more.

**Virtual ownership:** the effect activates even before formal ownership. "Imagine this is yours" framing, free trial periods, and test drives all trigger the endowment effect on items not yet owned.

**Implications:** trial periods are not just marketing — they create psychological ownership that makes reverting (canceling) feel like a loss.

## Option and Choice Overload

Beyond a threshold, more options reduce decision quality, satisfaction, and conversion.

**Jam study (Iyengar & Lepper):** 24-choice display → 3% purchase rate; 6-choice display → 30% purchase rate. 10× difference in conversion with 75% fewer options.

**Mechanisms:** (1) evaluation cost scales with option count; (2) anticipated regret increases — the more options forgone, the more the unchosen options are idealized; (3) opportunity cost of choosing any option is more salient.

**Paradox of choice:** having more options produces worse outcomes even when the optimal choice is available in both conditions.

## Procrastination and Present Bias

Humans systematically overvalue immediate rewards relative to future rewards — hyperbolic discounting rather than exponential discounting. The future self is psychologically treated as a different person.

**Hot-cold empathy gap:** in a cold state, we underestimate how much immediate desires (pain, hunger, arousal, social pressure) will influence behavior in a hot state. Self-binding contracts (Ulysses contracts) address this — commit in a cold state to constrain hot-state behavior.

**Retirement savings:** automatic enrollment overcomes present bias — people consistently fail to enroll when action is required, but rarely opt out once enrolled. Default = powerful choice architecture.

## Relativity and the Decoy Effect

Humans evaluate everything relatively, not absolutely. Value is perceived as comparison, not as a property of the object.

**Decoy effect:** introducing an inferior option (-A) that is clearly worse than option (A) but comparable to it makes (A) seem better — not just vs. -A, but vs. all alternatives including (B), which is harder to compare with (A) directly.

**The Economist experiment (MIT Sloan MBAs):**
- With three options (Online $59 / Print $125 / Print+Online $125): 16 chose online, 0 chose print, 84 chose print+online
- Without the print-only decoy: 68 chose online, 32 chose print+online
- The print-only option (which no one wanted) was the decoy that made print+online look like a bargain

**Mechanism:** we can't compare unlike things easily. A clearly inferior version of (A) gives us a comparison anchor that makes (A) win over (B) even though (A) vs. (B) was objectively difficult to evaluate. The decoy resolves ambiguity by creating an easy comparison.

**Applications:**
- Three-tier pricing: the middle option captures the most revenue by making the high tier look expensive and validating the middle vs. the low
- Restaurant anchoring: a $75 entrée makes the $45 item feel reasonable
- Real estate: showing two comparable properties plus a slightly inferior third drives selection toward the superior of the first two

## Dishonesty and Moral Reminders

Dishonesty follows a predictable pattern: people cheat a little — enough to benefit while maintaining a self-image as honest. Cheating is not calculated risk/reward; it is bounded by moral self-concept.

**Moral salience resets the threshold:** subjects who recalled the Ten Commandments before a math test (where inflation was possible) cheated significantly less — even non-religious subjects who recalled as many commandments as they could. The act of moral recall activated a moral frame.

**Honor codes work** — even when unenforceable — by making honesty salient as an identity commitment, not a constraint. MIT, Princeton, and similar institutions show reduced cheating rates with honor codes, not because consequences changed but because signing activates the social-norm frame.

**Implication:** behavioral checks (moral reminders, honor codes, in-the-moment attestations) reduce dishonesty more than surveillance or punishment escalation, because they shift the frame from market norms (cost/benefit) to social norms (identity).

## Expectations and Perception

Expectations alter the actual experience of a product, not just the evaluation. In blinded vs non-blinded tasting: wine described as expensive is experienced as tasting better even at a neurological level (fMRI shows different medial orbitofrontal cortex activation).

**Marketing is not just signaling — it changes the product.** The experience of a Coke in a Coke cup is genuinely different from the same liquid in an unlabeled cup. Brand is a product input.
