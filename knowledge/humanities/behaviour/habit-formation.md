# Habit Formation

[Source: WORK/BOOKS/Atomic_Habits.pdf]

**Central thesis:** Behavior change is not a matter of motivation or willpower — it's a matter of systems. Small 1% improvements compound to transformative outcomes; identity-based habits outperform outcome-based habits in durability.

## The Habit Loop

Four-stage loop: **Cue → Craving → Response → Reward**

- **Cue:** triggers the brain to initiate behavior; predicts a reward. Time, location, emotional state, preceding action, other people.
- **Craving:** motivational force; the desire for the reward the habit delivers. You don't crave the habit itself — you crave the state change it produces.
- **Response:** the actual behavior (thought or action). Constrained by friction (physical and cognitive effort required).
- **Reward:** satisfies the craving and teaches the brain the loop is worth repeating. Two functions: satisfying in the moment; teaching the brain which actions to remember.

Habits persist because they are encoded as efficient neural pathways. The brain stops fully deliberating and runs the routine automatically when the cue is detected.

## The Four Laws of Behavior Change

To build a habit: satisfy each law. To break a habit: invert each law.

| Law | Build | Break |
|-----|-------|-------|
| 1. Make it Obvious | cue visible, implementation intention, habit stacking | remove cue from environment |
| 2. Make it Attractive | temptation bundling, join culture where behavior is norm | reframe motivations, make unattractive |
| 3. Make it Easy | reduce friction, 2-minute rule, prime environment | increase friction, add barriers |
| 4. Make it Satisfying | immediate reward, habit tracking, never miss twice | make immediately unsatisfying, add accountability |

## 1% Compounding

1% improvement per day = 37× better at end of year (1.01^365 = 37.78). 1% worse per day = 0.03 of starting capability.

The compounding is invisible in the short term — which is why habits are hard to maintain before results appear. **The Plateau of Latent Potential:** progress accumulates invisibly until a threshold is crossed, then appears "sudden." Bamboo model — years of underground root growth before rapid visible growth.

## Identity-Based Habits

Two layers of change:
- **Outcome-based:** "I want to lose 20 lbs" — goal at the surface layer
- **Identity-based:** "I am the kind of person who moves every day" — belief at the core

The problem with outcome-based habits: they end when the goal is achieved. Identity-based habits are self-reinforcing — each instance is a vote for the identity → identity strengthens → more of the behavior → more votes.

**Formation:** every action is a vote for the identity you want to embody. Don't start by deciding who you want to be. Start by proving it to yourself with small wins.

## Implementation Intention

"I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]."

Explicit pre-commitment to a cue removes the decision point at execution time. Doubles habit completion rates in controlled studies. Specificity is the mechanism — vague intentions ("I'll exercise more") fail at cue detection.

## Habit Stacking

"After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]."

Chains a new habit onto an existing one. The existing habit's reward state primes execution of the new behavior. Best anchors: high-frequency habits already deeply encoded (morning coffee, arriving at desk, closing laptop for lunch).

## Environment Design

Behavior is a function of person + environment. Most people try to change behavior by changing willpower → high-effort, fails under stress. Changing the environment changes behavior with low effort.

**Visibility rule:** make the cue of the desired behavior prominent; reduce visibility of cues for unwanted behaviors. Fruit bowl on the counter vs. chips in an opaque cabinet.

**Context separation:** assign specific contexts to specific behaviors. Working at a desk in a dedicated room vs. working in bed → the bed becomes a cue for sleep, not work-avoidance.

## The 2-Minute Rule

When starting a new habit, scale it down to under 2 minutes of execution. "Read before bed" → "open the book." "Run 3×/week" → "put on running shoes."

The principle: showing up (behavioral execution) is the habit to form first. Duration and intensity are optimized later. A 2-minute habit that is performed consistently beats a 60-minute habit that is performed intermittently.

## Habit Tracking and "Never Miss Twice"

Visual tracking (habit chain) creates a streak — a secondary reward that makes not performing feel like a loss. **Never miss twice** is the practical rule: missing once is noise; missing twice begins forming a new (avoidance) habit.

Perfection is not the target — consistency is. "Keep the streak alive" incentive is weaker than "never miss twice" because streaks create pressure that leads to cheating (incomplete performances to maintain the chain).

## Cardinal Rule of Behavior Change

**What is immediately rewarded is repeated. What is immediately punished is avoided.**

The core challenge of most high-value habits: the costs are immediate, the rewards are delayed. The core problem of most bad habits: the costs are delayed, the rewards are immediate.

Design interventions must close the immediate-feedback gap: habit tracking (immediate satisfaction), accountability partners (immediate social cost for failure), identity stakes (immediate identity-consistency reward).
